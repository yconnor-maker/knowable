The Mental Model

Simplify. Simplify. Simplify.

I'm Yamicia Connor. I'm a physician — I deliver babies. I'm also a trained engineer. I founded the Labora Collective because the women's health crisis in this country is not what most people think it is, and I've spent the better part of a decade figuring out what it actually is.

There are so many things to talk about right now. It can feel like a mess of spaghetti in your head. Bad thing after bad thing after bad thing. A lot of what I spend my time doing — as a thinker — is figuring out how to bucket things. Because I'm a physician, yes, but I'm also a trained engineer. And engineers do one thing relentlessly: we simplify.

So this is the way we are simplifying it. A mental model. It walks you through the moral philosophy, the history, what's happening politically, what's happening legally, and how it's going to affect your health — step by step, between now and the midterms. This site is the seed. The full body of work — and we have years of it — sits underneath.

How We Know

Not from one source. From every angle we could find.

Before we tell you what we know, let us tell you how we know it. Because you're going to be told this is opinion. You're going to be told it's partisan. You're going to be told, "how do you know?"

We know because we did not look once. We looked thirty-three times. Our flagship report — The State of Women's and Children's Health — analyzes 33 dimensions of women's and children's well-being across all 50 states, cross-referenced against federal policy, judicial rulings, clinical outcomes, and historical patterns going back to Comstock, 1873. We built causal maps. We built dismantlement timelines. We walked through the case law. We watched maternity wards close in real time. We read the actual text of the legislation.

We have turned this over and over again, trying to unexplain it. Trying to find a more innocent reading. A benign cause. A different shape. Each time, the same shape emerges. That is how we know.

Question One

What do they want?

Three things. They've never been quiet about it among themselves — only in mixed company.

One: women back in the home.

Not metaphorically. Literally. A society where a woman cannot lease an apartment without her father or husband's signature — which was the law in most U.S. jurisdictions until the Equal Credit Opportunity Act of 1974. Female economic independence not as a right, but as an accident.

Two: Black Americans returned to a permanent underclass.

Voting rights gutted — Shelby County v. Holder, 2013. Affirmative action ended — SFFA v. Harvard, 2023. And in 2025–2026, the word Black itself stripped from federal health language across HHS, CDC, and HRSA.

Three: white men, and men in general, restored to dominant and unchallenged power.

Not earned — restored. The operative word is unchallenged. They're still winning by every economic measure. The grievance isn't losing. The grievance is being challenged. That's the unbearable thing. The work is to make sure it can't happen again.

Question Two

Why do they want it?

Because they believe it. This is the part most polite analysis refuses to say plainly: they believe these things. Not as code. As theology and as worldview.

They believe women are responsible for the destruction of society. Pastor Dale Partridge published a book in March 2026 called 19 Reasons to Repeal the 19th Amendment. Pastor Joel Webbon preaches that wives should vote how their husbands tell them to. Partridge writes that women have "suicidal empathy" and need male governance for their own good. The Secretary of Defense, Pete Hegseth, reshares these sermons approvingly. This is no longer the fringe. This is the operating belief of the men writing the laws.

They believe in the inferiority of Black people. Not as a historical artifact — as a present operating principle. Every dismantlement of federal civil rights infrastructure is the policy expression of a belief.

They believe the antisemitic tropes. That Jewish money controls the world. Globalist. Soros. The elites. They believe it.

Question Three

How are they doing it?

Two arms working in concert. The political arm builds. The legal arm cements.

The political arm has been running for sixty years.

Powell Memo, 1971. Heritage Foundation, 1973. Moral Majority, 1979. Federalist Society, 1982. They wrote the model legislation. They funded the candidates. They captured the state legislatures. Texas HB 7. Louisiana warrants for New York doctors. One statute at a time. That track has its own name: Manufactured Healthcare Crisis — already published, linked below.

The legal arm cements what the political arm builds.

Forty years of judicial pipeline produced the most anti-democratic Supreme Court since Reconstruction. Shelby, 2013. Hobby Lobby, 2014. Dobbs, 2022. SFFA, 2023. Loper Bright, 2024. Purl v. HHS vacating HIPAA reproductive privacy. That track launches next: Bring The Bolt Cutters — twenty-four receipts, one every Friday, until the midterms.

Question Four

Why are they wrong?

This is the question I care about most as a Catholic, a physician, and a thinker. And I want to be honest about it — there are parts of their argument we actually agree with. Just not the conclusion.

Because the original pro-life position was not what the religious right turned it into. It was a serious moral philosophy. Let me start at the beginning.

Natural law: the foundation.

Thomas Aquinas, thirteenth century, building on Aristotle. The claim: there is an objective moral order woven into reality itself, and human reason can know it. Human dignity is not a feeling. It is a fact about what we are.

The seamless garment.

From that foundation: if human dignity is real, you cannot defend it selectively. Every threat to human life is forbidden by the same logic. Abortion. Capital punishment. War. Poverty. Racism. Segregation. You can't pick. Eileen Egan coined the phrase in 1971, drawing from the Gospel of John — the robe of Christ the Roman soldiers refused to tear. Cardinal Bernardin formalized it at Fordham, December 1983. Feminists for Life formed in 1972 on this premise: support the woman, and she will choose the child. The original Catholic position was progressive on economics, anti-coercion, anti-death-penalty, anti-war, pro-mother. It was bipartisan. It was a fringe of radical Catholics. And it is everything the modern political "pro-life" movement is not.

The same logic produced Dr. King.

The same natural law tradition produced Martin Luther King Jr.'s argument against segregation. Letter from Birmingham Jail, April 16, 1963. King wrote:

An unjust law is a human law that is not rooted in eternal law and natural law. Any law that uplifts human personality is just. Any law that degrades human personality is unjust. All segregation statutes are unjust because segregation distorts the soul and damages the personality.

That is King. Citing Aquinas. By name. In jail.

Black Baptist preacher in 1963. Italian-American Catholic Cardinal in 1983. Catholic pacifist activist in 1971. Same foundation. Same logic. Same garment.

What actually happened in 1979.

Paul Weyrich and Jerry Falwell sat down to build a political movement. They needed a respectable cover story. What they were actually defending was the tax-exempt status of Bob Jones University — revoked by Nixon's IRS in 1976 because Bob Jones banned interracial dating, a ban they didn't lift until the year 2000. But "we want our segregated schools back" was not, by 1979, a slogan you could run on. So they reached for the Catholic anti-abortion cause that was already lying around.

Here's the receipt — abortion used to be bipartisan. The Southern Baptist Convention passed pro-choice resolutions in 1971, 1974, and 1976. Baptist Press called Roe a victory for "religious liberty, human equality and justice" nine days after the decision. Jerry Falwell did not preach a single sermon against abortion until February 1978 — five years after Roe. The historian Randall Balmer documented this. Paul Weyrich admitted it on record at a 1990 religious-right conference. Grover Norquist confirms it.

They took the conclusion. They could not take the philosophy.

Because the philosophy — natural law applied consistently — would have forbidden what they were defending. Segregation was unjust under natural law. King had said so. Aquinas had said so. If they had adopted the actual Catholic position, they would have been arguing against themselves. So they kept the slogan and threw the reasoning away. They kept "abortion is wrong." They dropped "capital punishment is wrong, war is wrong, segregation is wrong, poverty is wrong, mothers must be supported." They built a pro-life movement that is anti-mother. They kept the punishment. They dropped the safety net.

What Was Life Before Roe

The sepsis wards.

You cannot understand Roe without understanding what came before it. I have an attending — one of the senior physicians who trained me. She started her career before Roe. She tells me about sepsis rounds. These were literal rounds, on wards full of women dying from botched illegal abortions and complicated miscarriages they couldn't get treated. She holds the memory of standing at bedsides holding women's hands while they died. Agonizing deaths. From medically treatable conditions, in a country where treating them was a crime.

That is the medical reality Roe was written into. Roe was not abstract. It was a response to a body count. Every restriction now in force — every six-week ban, every miscarriage prosecution, every closed maternity ward — is rebuilding those sepsis wards. That's what the medical track — Crisis to Care — is about.
